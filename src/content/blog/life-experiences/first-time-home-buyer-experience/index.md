Buying a home for the first time is one of those things where you have absolutely no idea what you are getting into until you are already in it. I had never taken a loan before, had no experience with property registration, and honestly did not even know what stamp duty was before this process started. I had my uncle to help me navigate things, which saved me a lot of confusion. But even with that help, there were parts of this journey that genuinely tested my patience.

This is a walkthrough of how the whole thing went, including the parts that did not go smoothly at all. If you are thinking about buying your first home, hopefully this gives you a clearer picture of what to expect.

---

### Step 1: Eligibility Check Before Everything Else

The first thing I learned was to not look at a single flat before checking how much the bank would actually lend me. There is no point visiting properties and getting excited about something before you know what your budget is.

I went to SBI for an eligibility check. Based on my income, they told me I was eligible for around 35 lakhs. They gave me an In-Principle Approval Letter. This is not the final loan sanction. It just means the bank is open to lending you up to that amount, subject to your documents and the property checking out later. But it is enough to start looking at properties with a real number in mind.

---

### Step 2: Under Construction vs Ready to Move

Once I had a rough budget, I started looking at options. I had two broad choices in front of me.

**Option 1: Ready to Move flat**
You see exactly what you are buying. You can move in quickly. No waiting, no construction uncertainty. The downside is that your full EMI starts almost immediately after the loan is disbursed.

**Option 2: Under Construction property**
The flat is still being built. Possession in my case was 20 months away. The loan gets disbursed in tranches as construction progresses, and until the full amount is released you only pay something called a Pre-EMI, which is interest only on the amount disbursed so far. This is significantly lower than a full EMI. It buys you time to build some financial buffer before the full EMI kicks in.

I am early in my career and the lower outflow during construction was appealing. I went with under construction.

A couple of things worth knowing before making this choice:

**GST applies on under-construction properties but not on ready-to-move ones.** For affordable or budget housing like mine, the GST rate is 1%. For other properties it is 5%. Factor this into your total cost.

**Try to pick under-construction projects with possession within 2 years.** Beyond that you are taking on more risk around construction delays and builder reliability without enough benefit to justify it.

---

### Step 3: Booking the Flat

After enough site visits and back and forth, I found a place that worked for me. We agreed on a deal and I paid the initial token amount to book the unit. That locked it in and kicked off the formal process.

---

### Step 4: Stamp Duty and Registration

This was the first time I walked into a government office for something property related and genuinely had no idea what I was doing.

You visit the Sub-Registrar Office in your area. This is where the property ownership gets legally recorded in your name. The costs here are:

- **Stamp duty**: usually 6 to 7 percent of the property value
- **Registration fee**: a fixed amount
- **Lawyer fees**: around 15,000 to 20,000 for the legal assistance on the day

This is cash you need upfront, separate from the loan. A lot of first-time buyers are surprised by how significant this outflow is. Plan for it well in advance.

Once registration was done, I had the original property documents in hand and was ready to submit everything to the bank.

---

### Step 5: Document Submission and Bank Verifications

The document list is long but straightforward once you know what is needed.

**From my side:**
Salary slips, salary account bank statement, offer letter, increment letters, Form 26AS, EPFO passbook, ID and address proof.

**From the builder's side:**
Their NOC, a demand letter for the first tranche, receipts of payments I had already made, and the original stamp duty and registration documents.

There was one more document on the builder's side that I did not know about until it became a problem. The ROC/NOC from the builder's partner bank, which is a No Objection Certificate for your specific unit issued by the bank that has financed the builder's project. I will come back to this.

Once documents were submitted, SBI started their verification rounds. An agent came home to verify my address. Someone visited my office to verify my employment. An engineer visited the construction site for a technical inspection. A lawyer prepared a legal opinion on the property. For the technical and legal reports, I paid a separate fee directly to the parties creating them. These are not included in the loan processing fee.

After all of this, I received the Final Sanction Letter. This is the real one. It has the approved loan amount, tenure, and interest rate. Then I went to the SBI Home Loan Processing Centre with all original documents for the final submission and disbursal.

And that is where things started to fall apart a bit.

---

### Step 6: The ROC Document and One and a Half Months of Chaos

At the processing centre, everything looked in order. Disbursal was the last step. The bank then asked for the ROC/NOC from the builder's partner bank for my specific unit. I did not have it.

I reached out to the builder's RM. The response was vague. Somewhere between "it will come soon" and "the bank releases these in batches, might take 10 to 15 days." I said okay and waited.

Meanwhile the builder's automated system had no clue any of this was happening. Payment reminders kept coming. Late interest kept getting added to my dues. At one point I received a cancellation intimation for an issue that was entirely from the builder's side, not mine. I raised this with them, got it acknowledged in writing, and was promised a waiver on the interest that had accumulated because of their delay.

The document arrived after one and a half months. Not 10 to 15 days. A month and a half of repeated follow-ups, messages, and phone calls.

I submitted it to SBI immediately. By this point a second tranche was also due, so I shared the updated demand letter from the builder as a digital copy along with everything else.

The bank came back and said they needed a hard copy with stamp and signature for the first disbursal. I asked why the digital copy was not enough. They said it is their policy for the first disbursal. Fine. I got the hard copy from the builder's office and gave it to them.

Then came the next issue. The bank said the signature on the builder's agreement did not match the signature on the builder's NOC. They needed to verify who actually signed it.

I got a Board Resolution document from the builder listing all authorised signatories and submitted that. The bank said that was not enough because they had no other document with that person's signature to cross-reference against.

At this point I got both the builder's RM and the SBI banker on a call together. They argued. The call was not particularly productive. Eventually the RM took the banker's number and connected him with another SBI banker from their partner branch to sort it out directly. The final resolution: SBI asked the builder's RM to send an email from the official company email confirming the document was signed and issued by them. The RM sent the email. That was it. That was all it took after all of that.

The first disbursal happened.

---

### What I Would Tell Someone Starting This

Looking back, the process itself is not complicated. What makes it feel that way is that multiple parties are involved and you are completely dependent on each of them moving at their own pace. You can do your part right and still be stuck waiting on someone else. That is just how it goes.

A few things worth knowing before you start:

Ask the builder upfront when the ROC/NOC from their partner bank will be available for your unit. If the answer is vague, push harder before you book. This single document was responsible for most of my delay.

Keep a written record of every commitment and acknowledgement. When the cancellation notice came in for a builder-side issue, having their written acknowledgement was what sorted it out.

Expect the bank to surface new requirements at different stages. Not everything gets communicated upfront. The disbursal stage in particular can drag longer than you expect.

And the mental load is real. The paperwork itself is fine. It is the waiting, the follow-ups, the circular conversations between builder and bank that drain you. Even when you are doing everything right, it does not always feel like it.

But it gets done. The first disbursal came through eventually, and whatever chaos came with it is now just a story.
